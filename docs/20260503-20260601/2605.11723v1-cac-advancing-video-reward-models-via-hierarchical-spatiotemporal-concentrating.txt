Abstract 

Recently, video generation models have achieved remarkable progress in global visual fidelity. However, when synthesizing complex dynamic content, they fre-quently produce sparse subtle anomalies . Existing video reward models primarily emphasize overall visual quality and text alignment; yet as generation quality improves, these coarse-grained defects become less frequent, while the sparse structural, temporal, and physical anomalies become an increasingly important bottleneck. Therefore, in this paper, we propose Concentrate and Concentrate (CaC), a coarse-to-fine anomaly reward model based on Vision-Language Models. During inference, it first conducts a global temporal scan to anchor anomalous time windows, then performs fine-grained spatial grounding within the localized interval, and finally derives robust judgments via structured spatiotemporal Chain-of-Thought reasoning. To equip the model with these capabilities, we construct the first large-scale generated video anomaly dataset with per-frame bounding-box annotations, temporal anomaly windows, and fine-grained attribution labels. Build-ing on this dataset, we design a three-stage progressive training paradigm. The model initially learns spatial and temporal anchoring through single- and multi-frame supervised fine-tuning, and then is optimized by a reinforcement learning strategy based on two-turn Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO). Beyond conventional accuracy rewards, we introduce Temporal and Spatial IoU rewards to supervise the intermediate localization process, effectively guiding the model toward more grounded and interpretable spatiotemporal reasoning. Extensive ex-periments demonstrate that CaC can stably “concentrate” on subtle anomalies, achieving a 25.7% accuracy improvement on fine-grained anomaly benchmarks and, when used as a reward signal, CaC reduces generated-video anomalies by 11.7% while improving overall video quality.

1 Introduction 

Recent advances in video generation models [ 5, 40 , 42 , 22 , 14 , 29 ] have significantly improved the visual fidelity of generated videos. However, when producing complex and dynamic content, these models still frequently exhibit hallucinations [ 17 , 18 ], including structural distortions [ 13 , 55 ], physical-law violations [ 57 , 6], temporal inconsistencies [ 4, 33 ], and corrupted text rendering [ 28 , 10 ]. Unlike the well-defined anomalies observed in other areas, anomalies in generated videos are far more complex and often manifest as subtle violations of physical/logical priors. Recently, the reinforcement learning (RL) paradigm has significantly enhanced the capabilities of generative models by employing reward models to evaluate outputs and drive optimization. In the domain of video generation, there is a critical need for a robust reward model capable of providing stable and accurate feedback on video anomalies, thereby advancing video generation quality. While existing video reward models [ 54 , 52 , 56 , 60 , 16 , 50 ] play a pivotal role in aligning human pref-erences, they still largely emphasize visual quality and text alignment, leaving structural distortions and localization errors relatively under-explored. Unlike global visual quality issues, hallucinations in generated videos are often spatiotemporally sparse, appearing only in a few frames or occupying small localized regions. Consequently, identifying these subtle errors requires fine-grained spatiotem-poral perception to isolate weak anomaly signals from large amounts of normal context, yet these faint signals are often overwhelmed by current models’ global video-level reasoning. To bridge this gap, video reward modeling requires a dedicated mechanism that can actively concentrate on these sparse anomalies through spatiotemporal focusing, filtering out the vast normal context to enable fine-grained reasoning. In this paper, we propose Concentrate and Concentrate (CaC) , a VLM-based reward model with a coarse-to-fine two-turn localization strategy for focusing on subtle anomalies. During inference, CaC first conducts a global scan to anchor the anomalous temporal range, followed by fine-grained spatial grounding within the selected time window. This design not only reduces the burden of spatiotemporal search, but also mitigates VLM hallucinations by deriving final judgments from interpretable spatiotemporal CoT reasoning. To enable this coarse-to-fine reasoning ability, we construct a three-stage training framework. First, we train the model to recognize and localize anomalies. Given single frames extracted from videos, the model learns anomaly comprehension and bbox grounding, laying a foundation for subsequent video-level detection. Second, we extend training to multi-frame inputs, enabling the model to capture temporal errors in dynamic scenes. This stage jointly trains global video scanning and fine-grained video-clip localization, ensuring that the model outputs the formats required for two-turn interaction. Finally, we employ two-turn GRPO to optimize the VLM’s reasoning trajectory, enabling it to produce more stable and accurate judgments. We design a diversified reward mechanism, including format verification, temporal IoU, spatial IoU, attribution IoU, and result accuracy, which guides the model toward more accurate spatiotemporal localization and interpretable anomaly judgment. To support the above pipeline, we construct the first large-scale dataset for sparse anomalies in generated videos, with expert annotations of temporal segments, anomaly types, and per-frame bboxes . Although labor-intensive, these annotations are crucial: 1) they improve CoT ground-truth (GT) construction by allowing video clips with GT spatiotemporal annotations to be fed into the foundation model, effectively reducing hallucinations during label generation compared with prior work [ 16 , 54 ]; 2) they provide references for attribution and temporal/spatial IoU rewards in the third training stage, guiding the model toward more interpretable reasoning through explicit localization. Extensive experiments on our anomaly benchmark demonstrate that CaC reliably detects sparse anomalies, achieving 81.7% overall accuracy and improving over the strongest baseline by 25.7%. On a harder split with increasingly sparse anomalies, CaC’s advantage grows as anomalies become shorter in duration and smaller in spatial extent. CaC also improves accuracy by 19.9% on the external Skyra-derived subset, providing an out-of-domain generalization. More importantly, when used as a reward signal for downstream video generation, CaC reduces generated-video anomalies by 11.7% and improves overall video quality, demonstrating that its judgments provide actionable optimization feedback. Our contributions can be summarized as follows: • We construct the first large-scale comprehensive dataset for sparse anomalies in generated videos, providing anomaly types, reasons, temporal windows, and per-frame bboxes. These annotations support high-quality CoT synthesis for SFT and provide verifiable reward signals for the RL stage. 2• We propose CaC, a coarse-to-fine two-turn reward model that recognizes sparse video anomalies through hierarchical spatiotemporal focusing. Its structured judgments can be converted into reward feedback for reducing anomalies in generated videos. • We develop a three-stage progressive pipeline for CaC training. In the RL stage, we further introduce temporal, spatial, and attribution IoU rewards to guide the VLM toward grounded, interpretable anomaly CoT reasoning and stable judgments. 

2 Related Work 

Video Reward Models. With the development of generative models [ 46 , 45 , 47 , 48 ], reward modeling has become a key technique for aligning them with human preferences. Early single-scalar approaches [ 59 , 21 , 30 ] are insufficient for capturing multi-faceted visual quality. Subsequent video-domain methods incorporate human-annotated ratings [ 23 , 15 ] and Bradley–Terry loss [ 27 , 32 ], with some frameworks unifying cross-task evaluation [ 53 ] or extending to multi-dimensional scoring [ 16 ,51 , 27 ]. More recent VLM-based reward models further integrate CoT reasoning [ 52 ], dynamic sampling, or visual rechecking [ 54 , 50 ] to improve evaluation depth. However, localized structural and physical anomalies can still be overwhelmed by predominantly normal context. In contrast, CaC introduces a coarse-to-fine spatiotemporal grounding mechanism, effectively concentrating on sparse anomalies amid normal context. 

Reinforcement Learning. The integration of RL [ 25 ] into LLMs and MLLMs [ 8 , 49 , 2 ] has significantly advanced their reasoning capabilities [ 58 , 31 , 39 ]. While early implementations relied on PPO [ 34 ], GRPO [ 35 , 12 ] simplifies advantage estimation via group-relative baselines and leverages verifiable rewards to elicit long-chain reasoning without extensive preference data. Recently, GRPO has been further applied to visual understanding [ 53 , 60 , 50 , 52 , 37 ] and visual generation [ 61 , 44 , 26 ]. In this work, we use LLM-style GRPO to train the CaC reward model, while downstream video alignment follows generation-style GRPO. 

Video Anomaly Detection. This traditional CV task primarily studies abnormal events in real-world surveillance videos, such as human, vehicle, and environment anomalies [ 1 ]. This setting is fundamentally different from our generated-video anomalies studied here: our anomalies refer to structural distortions, temporal inconsistencies, etc. A few recent benchmarks and evaluators study these anomalies in generated videos [ 18 , 3 , 54 ], but they typically focus on video-level judgments or rule-level assessment rather than fine-grained spatiotemporal anomalies. Additionally, detector-style methods [ 37 , 24 ] discriminate real from AI-generated videos via artifact reasoning. Though their default task differs from ours, we still include them as baselines and leverage their data for out-of-domain evaluation given the scarcity of open-source generated-video artifact models. 

Visual Focusing. This line of work aims to allocate computation to informative regions or moments for fine-grained visual understanding. Existing studies explore image-level zooming and cropping [ 7,36 ], frame-level keyframe selection for long-video understanding [ 62 , 63 ], and spatiotemporal grounding as a reference basis [ 43 , 41 ]. These works improve general visual understanding or target grounding, but typically stop at perception or localization. In contrast, CaC focuses on sparse anomalies in generated videos and, after temporal focusing and spatial grounding, further converts the anomaly evidence into actionable reward feedback for improving video generation quality.

We manually screen the outputs and curate ∼25K videos covering broad anomaly types. CoT Synthesis with Human Annotation. High-quality CoT data can improve the effectiveness of reward models [ 16 ]. To construct such data for sparse video anomalies, we design an annota-tion pipeline that combines expert human labels with VLM-based CoT synthesis, extending prior practices [ 37 , 38 , 52 ]. As shown in Fig. 2 (left), we uniformly downsample each video into frame sequences, which are examined frame by frame by human experts. For anomalous samples, annotators label the temporal segments, anomaly types, underlying reasons, and affected-region bboxes. Given that subtle anomalies are difficult to annotate accurately, we organize 50 video evaluation experts, including 40 annotators and 10 reviewers, and adopt a two-round review mechanism where errors are returned for correction. We further randomly check 10% of the annotations to verify bbox and anomaly-type accuracy. Detailed annotation procedures are provided in Appendix B.1. Based on these annotations, as shown in Fig. 2 (bottom), we feed the original video, human-annotated GT, and cropped anomalous frames into Gemini-2.5-Pro [ 9], and design a four-step reasoning template to encourage comprehensive VLM analysis. Since some sparse anomalies are extremely difficult for VLMs to recognize, we introduce a generate-verify process, where the generated CoT and human annotations are sent back to Gemini to check their consistency. Samples verified within three rounds are deemed salient anomalies ; those verified within five rounds are deemed non-salient anomalies ;and the remaining samples are discarded, as they may be overly difficult or affected by human annotation errors. Ultimately, we retain 15,374 anomalous training samples. The detailed prompt templates are provided in Appendix B.2. We further pair the anomalous set with an equal number of normal videos constructed using the same pipeline, resulting in a dataset of approximately 30K videos. More dataset details are provided in Appendix B.3, with additional statistics visualized in Fig. S1.

We therefore first teach the VLM to recognize and localize these distortions from anomalous frames. As shown in Fig. 3 (Stage 1), each training sample contains an extracted anomalous frame, the video prompt, and the corresponding CoT reasoning sequence constructed in Sec. 3.1. Through this stage, the model acquires a structured CoT format and establishes a mapping from visual cues to anomaly attribution and spatial grounding.

3.4 Two-turn Inference Strategy for Hierarchical Concentrating 

Existing video reward models typically sample frames at fixed temporal intervals, feeding all sampled frames into the model simultaneously for inference. However, capturing subtle or fleeting anoma-lies inherently demands denser sampling, which incurs prohibitive computational overhead when processing long videos. In this work, we propose the Coarse-to-Fine Two-turn Inference strategy to efficiently localize anomalies. First, the input video is uniformly downsampled to obtain a sparse frame sequence (4 fps), which is fed into the VLM for global temporal scanning. The model initially detects anomalies in the video; if detected, it outputs the anomalous time window, along with its attribution analysis. Second, we extract the video clip corresponding to the predicted time window and densely resample it (8 fps). This clip is then fed back into the VLM for fine-grained spatial reasoning, where the model confirms the anomalies and outputs the anomaly attribution and spatial bboxes. The first turn therefore proposes a candidate anomalous window, while the second turn makes the final anomaly decision. Reward Interface. CaC is used as a reward model through a structured-to-scalar interface. Given a video v and its instruction p, CaC produces a structured response, and we convert the final judgment into a smooth scalar reward using the VLM’s vocabulary probabilities. Specifically, we read the probabilities of “normal” and “abnormal”, denoted as Pnorm and Pabn , and define: 

RCaC (v, p ) = Pnorm /(Pnorm + Pabn ). (7) Thus, CaC assigns higher rewards to likely normal videos and lower rewards to likely anomalous ones. The structured CoT, type, time window, and bbox can serve as interpretable evidence. 

4 Experiment 

Implementation. We adopt Qwen3-VL-8B [ 2 ] as the foundational model for CaC. All training stages unfreeze the full pipeline, including the ViT encoder, the adapter, and the LLM. In the warmup stage, we fine-tune the model with LoRA (rank =32) at a learning rate of 1 × 10 −4. In the Multi-frame SFT stage, we switch to full-parameter tuning with the same learning rate. In the RL stage, we perform two-turn GRPO for 250 steps with a learning rate of 1.0 × 10 −6, a rollout group size of 

G = 8 , a batch size of 64, and gradient accumulation over 32 steps. Further implementation details are provided in Appendix C.2. 

Benchmark. Existing benchmarks for video reward models [ 20 , 27 ] primarily evaluate aesthetics and text-video alignment via pairwise preference accuracy, differing from our task in both evaluation dimensions and protocols. We therefore construct CaC-Bench . This evaluation set is separated from the training data in videos, prompts, anomaly distribution, and fine-grained taxonomy, and contains two subsets: (1) CaC-Bench-Main (500 normal + 500 anomalous videos), human-annotated with anomaly types and causes, all cross-verified; (2) CaC-Bench-Hard (400 anomalous-only videos), categorized by temporal duration ( <1s / ≥1s) and spatial extent ( <20% / ≥20% of frame area) into four difficulty levels to stress-test model sensitivity. We also evaluate on an external annotated subset derived from open-source Skyra/ViF-CoT-4K data [ 24 ]. More benchmark construction details are provided in Appendix A. 

Baseline. On CaC-Bench-Main, we compare CaC with three groups of baselines: (1) leading general VLMs (Gemini-3.1-Pro [ 11 ], Gemini-2.5-Pro [ 9], GPT-4o [ 19 ], Qwen3-VL-8/32B [ 2]); (2) video reward models (UnifiedReward [ 53 ], UnifiedReward-Think [ 52 ], and VR-Thinker [ 50 ]); and (3) AIGC video detectors (VideoVeritas [ 37 ], Skyra-RL [ 24 ]). We keep frame sampling and prompting/protocol conversion consistent across methods, with details in Appendix C.2. For CaC-Bench-Hard, which contains only anomalous videos, we exclude models that exhibit strong prediction bias on CaC-Bench-Main ( e.g., Gemini-2.5-Pro variants that default to “abnormal”) and retain only those with reasonably balanced detection behavior for a fair comparison.

4.3 Ablation Results Effectiveness of the Progressive Training Paradigm. As shown in Tab. 3 ① vs. ②③④ vs. ⑩, all three training stages contribute substantially to the final performance and are jointly indispensable. In particular, ② removes the pre-training stage and reduces the overall accuracy by 12%, indicating that initial anomaly grounding provides an important foundation. ③ removes the SFT stage, which weakens the model’s ability to output correct formats, makes the RL stage unstable, and reduces accuracy by 16%. ④ disables the RL stage and leads to a significant performance degradation with a 14% drop. Together, these results confirm the necessity of the progressive three-stage training paradigm. 

Effectiveness and Efficiency of the Inference Strategy. As shown in Tab. 3 ⑤ vs. ①, when applying our two-turn CoT strategy to Qwen3-VL-8B, even in a training-free setting, it still marginally outperforms single-turn inference. In ⑥, CaC is retrained with a single-turn formulation that performs temporal and spatial localization simultaneously. Compared to ⑩, its accuracy drops by 8.3%. Taken together, both ⑤ and ⑥ validate the effectiveness of our inference strategy, suggesting that without the densified second-stage spatial grounding, the model struggles to reliably localize subtle anomalies. Compute analysis is provided in Appendix C.3 and Table S2. We acknowledge that this two-turn strategy doubles the reward-model inference time, but this overhead remains acceptable in video RL training, as our downstream experiments show that video sampling is more than 8 × slower than reward evaluation. Importantly, the added time cost yields a clear accuracy gain (for anomaly judgment, 56% accuracy is insufficient for reliable use), resulting in a substantially better performance-to-cost trade-off at the full training-system scale. 

Effectiveness of Individual Rewards. As shown in Tab. 3 ⑦⑧⑨ vs. ⑩, we remove the Tempo-ral/Spatial/Attribution IoU reward, respectively (the effectiveness of JSON-format/status rewards has been widely validated in prior work [ 53 ], so we omit them here). Among them, the Temporal IoU reward is the most critical: removing it causes a 19% performance drop because it directly determines the decision range of the second turn. These ablations collectively show that using process-level rewards to guide the model toward high-quality spatiotemporal CoT reasoning leads to more accurate anomaly judgment. 

5 Conclusion 

In this paper, we presented CaC (Concentrate and Concentrate), a coarse-to-fine reward model for detecting and localizing subtle anomalies in generated videos. By combining a two-turn inference strategy, progressive three-stage training, and verifiable spatiotemporal rewards, CaC mitigates the attention dilution caused by the extreme sparsity of video anomalies and produces more reliable grounded judgments. We further introduced a dataset with per-frame bbox annotations to support both training and evaluation in this setting. A.1 CaC-Bench Construction Details 

CaC-Bench is constructed as an independent evaluation suite for sparse anomaly detection in gen-erated videos. It is separated from the training data in videos, prompts, anomaly distribution, and fine-grained taxonomy, and is never used for SFT, GRPO, prompt selection, reward-weight selec-tion, or hyperparameter tuning. The goal is to evaluate whether a reward model can identify subtle localized structural, temporal, and physical anomalies under a test distribution that is distinct from the training stages. Specifically, unlike the data construction in Sec. 3.1, we collect a new set of 5K videos for CaC-Bench and do not require annotators to use the taxonomy in Fig. 2. CaC-Bench-Main contains 1,000 generated videos, balanced between 500 normal and 500 anomalous samples. For anomalous samples, annotators record the anomaly type and the underlying cause, and the annotations are cross-verified before inclusion. Normal samples are also selected only when no clear localized structural, temporal, physical, or character anomaly is observed after two rounds of verification. A.2 External Evaluation on a Skyra Annotated Subset 

To the best of our knowledge, there is currently no open-source benchmark specifically designed for generated-video anomaly detection. To provide an additional out-of-domain check beyond CaC-Bench and more fairly assess CaC’s performance and generalization, we construct an auxiliary external evaluation subset from the open-source Skyra-ViF-CoT-4K data [ 24 ]. Skyra studies AI-generated video detection through grounded artifact reasoning, i.e., , determining whether a video is AI-generated, and its annotated training data records human-perceivable visual artifacts in generated videos. These artifact categories are closely aligned with our anomaly taxonomy: person inconsis-tency corresponds to Human Distortion, text artifacts correspond to Character Anomalies, object inconsistency corresponds to Object Distortion, physics violations correspond to Physical Violations, and motion artifacts correspond to Motion Anomalies. The Skyra-ViF-CoT-4K subset is not intended to replace CaC-Bench, since CaC-Bench focuses on sparse anomalies. Instead, it evaluates whether models can detect annotated artifacts from an independent data source while maintaining low false positives on normal videos. The subset is balanced between anomalous and normal samples, and all methods are evaluated under the same frame-sampling setting and binary normal/abnormal parsing protocol. It is worth noting that we do not use ViF-Bench because it only provides real/synthetic video labels and does not include anomaly annotations. We also do not compare against Skyra-RL because this auxiliary test subset is derived from its training set. As shown in Table S1, existing general VLMs and other reward models remain close to random performance on this external subset, with either conservative normal-biased predictions or weak abnormal recall. In contrast, CaC achieves the best accuracy, anomalous F1, and normal F1, indicating that its anomaly-sensitive reasoning generalizes beyond CaC-Bench to artifacts annotated by an independent data source. 

B Dataset Construction and Details 

B.1 Detailed Annotation Guidelines 

We provide the complete annotation protocol for the CaC dataset, which comprises four stages: 

frame extraction and inspection , anomaly structured descriptions , bounding box grounding ,and quality assurance . We assemble a dedicated team of 50 video evaluation experts (40 annotators and 10 senior reviewers), all of whom complete a calibration session with exemplar videos covering our five predefined anomaly categories prior to formal annotation. The protocol operates on generated videos and does not rely on personal user metadata. 16 B.1.1 Frame Extraction and Inspection 

As described in Sec. 3.1, each video is first uniformly downsampled to extract a sequence of frames. Human experts then inspect each frame sequentially to detect anomalies. This frame-level examination ensures that both single-frame defects and multi-frame anomalies are captured. Videos in which no anomaly is identified in any frame are labeled as normal samples. 

B.1.2 Anomaly Structured Description 

For each of the five predefined categories in our taxonomy, annotators determine whether the anomaly type is present via binary classification. A single video may be associated with multiple categories simultaneously. The five categories are: Object Distortion (deformation, mutation, or clipping artifacts in inanimate objects), Human Distortion (limb deformity, abnormal fingers, extra limbs, or body splitting in human subjects), Motion Anomalies (unnatural trajectories, jitter/flicker, or sudden disappearance/appearance of entities across frames), Physical Violations (unsupported floating, interpenetration, or other behaviors contradicting real-world physics), and Character Anomalies 

(unreadable text, abnormal layout, or inconsistent textual content across frames). 

Annotation Format. For each detected anomaly, annotators provide a structured description that encodes the frame range, the affected objects, the anomaly types, and the underlying reason. The temporal boundaries are recorded as start and end indices on the uniformly sampled frame sequence, rather than timestamps. This unified format enables frame-level grounding of each anomaly instance while the underlying reason field captures the semantic context behind the anomalies. When multiple anomaly instances belong to the same category, they are concatenated within a single group; anomalies belonging to different categories are recorded in separate groups following the category ordering defined in the taxonomy. This hierarchical scheme enables unambiguous parsing of annotations into per-category anomaly groups, each containing one or more frame-grounded instances. 

Annotation Conventions. To resolve ambiguities during annotation, we establish several guiding principles. When an anomaly is detectable from a single frame ( e.g., , a deformed hand or unreadable text), it is assigned to a single-frame category (Object Distortion, Human Distortion, or Character Anomalies) even if it persists across multiple frames, whereas multi-frame categories (Motion Anomalies and Physical Violations) are reserved for anomalies whose identification inherently requires cross-frame comparison. To distinguish Object Distortion from Physical Violations, the former captures structural deformation of the object itself, while the latter targets implausible interactions between objects or with the environment. For Character Anomalies, annotators compare textual content across the full frame sequence when needed, and inconsistent text is annotated with the complete frame range over which the inconsistency is observable, even if each individual frame appears plausible in isolation. 

B.1.3 Bounding Box Grounding 

For each identified anomaly instance, annotators draw a tight bounding box around the anomalous region in the corresponding frames within the annotated frame range. Bounding boxes are stored as 

(xmin , y min , x max , y max ) in the Qwen-style normalized coordinate system, where each frame axis is mapped to the range [0 , 1000] . The following principles guide this process: • Each bounding box must fully encompass the relevant distorted region such that the anomaly can be identified solely from the visual content within the box, without relying on surrounding context. • Multiple bounding boxes may be assigned to a single anomaly instance when the corresponding distortion manifests in multiple disjoint regions within the same frame. • When partial occlusion occurs, annotators approximate the full extent of the affected area based on visible cues and structural priors. • Bounding boxes should be as tight as possible, avoiding unnecessary inclusion of irrelevant visual content to minimize interference from unrelated structures. • For multi-frame anomalies that involve entity state transitions ( e.g., , sudden disappearance, appear-ance, or abrupt transformation), bboxes are annotated on the boundary frames where the transition occurs. Specifically, for disappearance events, the bounding box is placed on the last frame in which the entity is still visible, enclosing its full extent at that moment. For sudden appearance events, the bounding box is placed on the first frame in which the entity becomes visible. This convention 17 anchors the bbox annotation to the most informative frame while the structured description captures the full frame range of the anomaly. To guarantee annotation reliability, we adopt a rigorous two-round review mechanism. In the first round, each completed annotation is independently verified by a senior reviewer, who examines the anomaly types, frame boundaries, bboxes, and underlying reasons; any inconsistencies are flagged and returned to the original annotator for correction, and this cycle repeats until the reviewer approves the annotation. In the second round, we randomly sample 10% of the accepted annotations for a spot-check audit conducted by a separate group of reviewers, verifying the accuracy and consistency of both the bboxes and the anomaly types. Only batches achieving an inter-annotator agreement rate above 95% are accepted into the final dataset; those falling below this threshold are returned for complete re-annotation. 

B.2 Prompt Templates for CoT Synthesis 

In this section, we provide a detailed overview of the prompt templates used throughout the CoT data construction process. As described in Sec. 3.1, we feed each video along with its manual annotations into Gemini-2.5-Pro [ 9] to synthesize structured reasoning chains. The core synthesis prompt is shown in Figs. S4 and S5. Specifically, we supply the annotated anomaly types, temporal spans, underlying reasons, and corresponding bboxes to guide the model toward the difficult regions while still requiring it to re-examine the visual evidence. The model is instructed to follow a four-step reasoning template: first performing detailed frame-level inspection, then analyzing cross-frame consistency and motion, then making an overall anomaly assessment with concrete attribution, and finally producing a structured JSON output with the anomaly status, frame range, label, reason, problem region, and per-frame bboxes. Although the ground-truth annotations are provided, they are treated as guidance rather than answers to be copied; the model must independently verify whether the visual content supports the annotated anomaly. This design reduces hallucinated rationales and yields higher-quality CoT labels for subtle anomalies that are difficult for VLMs to discover from the raw video alone. For the verification stage, the generated CoT is checked against the human annotations to ensure that the reasoning chain is not only plausible but also aligned with the verified labels and localization; the verification prompt is shown in Fig. S6. For anomaly presence and type predictions, we perform exact string matching against the ground-truth labels to determine correctness; for the temporal segments and spatial bounding boxes, we compute the Intersection-over-Union (IoU) between the predicted and ground-truth regions, and consider a prediction correct if the IoU exceeds 0.5. A sample passes verification only when all criteria are simultaneously satisfied. This verification is necessary because GT-conditioned generation may still produce inconsistent reasoning, incorrect attribution, or imprecise localization, especially for non-salient anomalies. The process is repeated for up to five rounds—samples passing within the first three rounds are deemed salient anomalies, those passing in the fourth or fifth round are deemed non-salient anomalies, and those failing all five rounds are discarded to avoid introducing noise. By incorporating detailed descriptions of all five anomaly categories from our taxonomy into both prompts, we ensure that the synthesized CoT data faithfully reflects the fine-grained spatiotemporal reasoning required for video anomaly detection, thereby providing high-quality supervision for our three-stage progressive training pipeline.

C.2 Training Implementation Details 

We adopt Qwen3-VL-8B [ 2] as the backbone of CaC and train the full model pipeline across all stages, including the vision encoder, adapter, and language model. The three training stages use disjoint data splits, and each stage keeps a 1:1 ratio between normal and anomalous samples. The first stage learns single-frame anomaly grounding from about 20K frames. The anomalous frames are sampled from 5K videos that contain single-frame recognizable anomalies, while normal frames are randomly sampled from normal videos. The second stage performs multi-frame SFT with about 20K full-video samples and 15K local clips. Full-video samples are collected from the CaC dataset to train global temporal localization. Clip-level samples are cropped from the full videos and restricted to within 1 second. For anomalous videos, we require the cropped clip to fully cover the annotated anomalous region, because partial crops of cross-frame inconsistency events may introduce ambiguous or incorrect labels. The third stage constructs 7.5K training instances for two-turn GRPO from a non-overlapping data split. For optimization and compute, the warmup stage uses LoRA with rank 32 and a learning rate of 

1 × 10 −4, and is trained for 9,330 steps on 8 NVIDIA H200 GPUs, taking about 11.5 hours. The multi-frame SFT stage switches to full-parameter tuning with the same learning rate, and is trained for 7K steps on 8 H200 GPUs, taking about 30.5 hours. The RL stage runs GRPO with a learning rate of 1.0 × 10 −6, rollout group size G = 8 , batch size 64, and gradient accumulation over 32 steps; it is trained for 250 steps on 16 H200 GPUs, taking about 42 hours. 

Baseline Evaluation Protocol. For all comparison methods, we first use the same prompt as CaC under the same frame-sampling setting to obtain a binary normal/abnormal decision. For reward and detector-style baselines, we additionally run their official inference prompts or scoring protocols described in the corresponding papers [ 53 , 52 , 37 , 50 , 24 ]. We report the stronger result between the unified anomaly-prompt evaluation and the official-protocol evaluation for each baseline. 

C.3 Inference Compute Analysis 

As shown in Table S2, compared with single-turn reward models, the two-stage strategy roughly doubles the reward-model inference time because the model is queried twice. This is a reasonable trade-off: CaC spends more inference time but improves the overall accuracy from the strongest baseline’s 0.560 to 0.817, yielding a 25.7-point gain. This overhead is also modest in video-generation RL, where video sampling typically dominates reward evaluation. In a representative training iteration, video sampling takes at least 4 minutes, whereas reward-model evaluation takes at most 1 minute. Thus, the additional verification cost does not dominate the end-to-end optimization budget.

E Limitations and Broader Impact 

CaC is designed as a specialist reward model for sparse structural, temporal, and physical anomalies in generated videos, and therefore should not be used as the only objective for downstream alignment. We have to combine CaC with UnifiedReward: the general reward preserves text-video alignment and holistic quality, while CaC provides complementary anomaly-sensitive feedback. However, we believe this is also a future direction: as video generation becomes increasingly realistic, reliable evaluation may require multiple expert reward models working together rather than a single monolithic reward. Due to limited computational resources, our experiments are still restricted in scale. Although our training already uses 16-card and 32-card H200-level settings for the most expensive stages, broader multi-seed studies, larger downstream alignment sweeps, and evaluation on substantially longer videos would require considerably more compute. We therefore leave more exhaustive scaling analysis to future work. The dataset construction also relies on fine-grained human annotations of frame ranges and bounding boxes. These annotations improve supervision quality, but they are costly and may still contain subjective ambiguity in borderline cases. We hope that releasing and further improving CaC can support future work on more automated fine-grained annotation pipelines, reducing the manual burden while preserving localization quality. Finally, although the current RL formulation obtains a continuous reward through token-probability-based optimization, the reward feedback is still applied at the generated-video level. While CaC predicts temporal windows and spatial boxes, the optimization signal is not yet immediately mapped back to the exact anomalous frames or regions in the generator’s denoising process. A promising future direction is to combine anomaly localization with more localized RL guidance, so that the predicted spatiotemporal boxes can directly indicate where and how generation should be corrected. From a broader-impact perspective, CaC can support safer and more reliable video-generation systems by exposing subtle structural, temporal, and physical artifacts that are often missed by holistic evaluators. At the same time, improving anomaly detection and reward feedback may also help optimize generators toward more realistic synthetic media.
